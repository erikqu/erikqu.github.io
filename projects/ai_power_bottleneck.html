The AI Power Bottleneck

January 18, 2026

If we had the software today to automate the entire US information workforce (approx. 64 million people [1]), could we plug it in?

We often worry about AI taking jobs, but we rarely do the math on the physics. The constraint isn't just intelligence; it's thermodynamics. Biology is hyper-efficient. Silicon is power-hungry.

1. The Energy Cost of "Running" a Workforce

To power 64 million human brains during a workday requires about 1.2 Gigawatts of metabolic energy [2]. To power an equivalent fleet of 64 million AI accelerators (like Nvidia's H100) requires roughly 45 Gigawatts [3].

Replacing the biological workforce requires a 37x increase in instantaneous power draw.

2. The Infrastructure Lag

45 GW is technically only ~3.5% of the total US grid capacity [4]. That sounds manageable, until you realize we have nowhere to put it.

This amount of power is equivalent to doubling the entire existing US data center footprint overnight [5]. The chips can be manufactured in months, but the power plants and substations take years.

We can print silicon faster than we can pour concrete.

Conclusion

The limit to total automation isn't the algorithm; it's the outlet.

To automate the US economy, we don't just need AGI. We need to build the electrical equivalent of a new city every month for the next decade. Until the electrons catch up to the neurons, humans remain the most energy-efficient intelligence engines on the planet.

The Verdict: Why We Can't Automate All Work (Yet)

Decision: IMPOSSIBLE before ~2035.

Even if we assume software capability arrives tomorrow (e.g., GPT-6 or Claude 5), the physical infrastructure simply cannot support a full replacement of the human workforce. The grid is the hard cap.
